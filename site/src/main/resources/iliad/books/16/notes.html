This is a natural inference from the remains found preserved at Myeenae, after three thousand years. And the following reminiscences of the custom appear in the Homeric poems: ταρχύω (ll. 456, 674; H 85) is undoubtedly another form of the later ταριχεύω, ‘embalm’ (cf. τάριχος, ‘mummy’), used by Herodotus, for example, in his description of the Egyptian process of mummifying (); the body of Patroclus was preserved by a sort of em balming, T 38, 39; the body of dead Hector was not burned until twenty-two days after his death (, 413, 664, 784), and Achilles's body was kept seventeen days (); jars of honey were laid away with the ashes of the dead (, ), the significance of which act lies in the fact that honey was anciently used as a preservative.—In Homeric times the dead were burned and their ashes, gathered in urns, were buried.
