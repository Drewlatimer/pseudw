    1. In the pre-Homeric days of Greece it is probable that bodies were embalmed (Helbig, Hom. Epos^{2}, pp. 51-56). This is a natural inference from the remains found preserved at Myeenae, after three thousand years. And the following reminiscences of the custom appear in the Homeric poems: ταρχύω (ll. 456, 674; H 85) is undoubtedly another form of the later ταριχεύω, ‘embalm’ (cf. τάριχοσ, ‘mummy’), used by Herodotus, for example, in his description of the Egyptian process of mummifying (); the body of Patroclus was preserved by a sort of em balming, T 38, 39; the body of dead Hector was not burned until twenty-two days after his death (, 413, 664, 784), and Achilles's body was kept seventeen days (); jars of honey were laid away with the ashes of the dead (, ), the significance of which act lies in the fact that honey was anciently used as a preservative.—In Homeric times the dead were burned and their ashes, gathered in urns, were buried. So ταρχύειν came to mean simply ‘bury.’ Over the ashes a mound of earth (τύμβοσ, l. 457) was raised, and surmounted by an upright gravestone (ςήλη, l. 457).— In historical times cremation seems to have given way largely, but by no means entirely, to ordinary interment.
